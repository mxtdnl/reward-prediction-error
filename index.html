Introduction

Watch the dopamine signal move.

Dopamine neurons don't report reward — they report surprise: the gap between the reward you get and the reward you predicted. As a cue learns to predict the reward, that signal migrates from the reward to the cue. This instrument lets you run the classic experiment and watch it happen.

The science — Schultz's hypothesis & findings

The hypothesis. Midbrain dopamine neurons were long assumed to signal reward. Wolfram Schultz's single-neuron recordings — formalised with Dayan & Montague (Science, 1997) — reframed that: they encode the reward-prediction error, δ = (reward received − reward predicted). This is the same error term that drives temporal-difference reinforcement learning, so the biology and the computational theory turned out to be one and the same.

  1. Unpredicted reward → a phasic burst (positive δ). A surprise reward excites the neuron.
  2. Predicted reward → once a cue reliably precedes reward, the burst transfers backward in time to the cue; at reward time the response vanishes (δ ≈ 0).
  3. Omitted reward → withholding a predicted reward makes firing dip below baseline at the expected time (negative δ).

Why it matters. Dopamine is a teaching signal, not a "pleasure" signal. It explains how neutral cues acquire motivational pull, grounds computational models of learning, and is central to reinforcement, motivation, and addiction (drugs that spike dopamine produce a prediction error that never fully extinguishes).

Schultz, W., Dayan, P. & Montague, P. R. (1997). A neural substrate of prediction and reward. Science, 275(5306), 1593–1599.

The idea — why this is the same signal

Each quarter you hold an estimate V of a channel's value. The return you get is compared with that expectation, and the gap — δ = return − V — is the reward-prediction error. Your estimate moves by V ← V + α·δ, exactly the update the dopamine signal implements in the brain.

The pay-off you feel (the return) and the thing that teaches you (δ) are different quantities. A well-learned channel keeps paying out while its δ — and the dopamine burst — falls silent; a channel that suddenly disappoints fires a negative error that pulls your estimate back down. That dissociation is the whole point: dopamine is a teaching signal, not a pleasure meter. The flashy pitch on each card is a cue — and, as in life, it doesn't reliably track true value. Only the prediction error does.
